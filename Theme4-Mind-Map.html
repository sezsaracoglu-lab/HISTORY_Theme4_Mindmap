Theme 4 · History & Methods of Psychology

The Logic & Ethos of Scientific Knowledge

how scientific knowledge is built, justified — and policed
What science is Objectivity & truth Reasoning tools Theory · law · cause · cycle The ethos (CUDOS)
Themes 1–3 asked what the mind is and what exists (ontology) — from the founding of psychology to the mind–body tetrad. Theme 4 turns the question around: not what is there, but how do we know it? This is epistemology — and the special case of scientific knowledge.
1
What is scientific knowledge?unification · reduction · common sense vs. science · 6 characteristics

Science is "organised common sense" (Nagel) — not a different kind of thinking, but a continuum: more systematic, general, explicit and open. Its great triumph is unification: explaining disconnected phenomena as effects of shared underlying causes.

Core movesUnification & reduction

Unification: many phenomena fitted under one framework (Mendeleyev's table; Newton's mechanics).

Reduction: explain a level by a deeper one — water = H₂O, thoughts = physiological mechanisms. The most distinguishing feature of science.

Two imagesCommon sense vs. science

Sellars: the manifest image (everyday objects & persons) vs. the scientific image (particles & forces).

Not a sharp divide but a continuum; Sellars wants both in "stereoscopic" view — both are real.

The 6 characteristics of scientific method (Nagel)

1 Systematicity 2 Well-defined methods 3 Reduction 4 Objectivity (intersubjective, replicable) 5 Clarity (unambiguous, public) 6 Revisability (never definitive)
2
Can knowledge be objective?realism · idealism / relativism · pragmatism

Knowledge = justified true belief. But what justifies it — the known (the object) or the knower (the mind)? Two extremes, and an intermediate position the authors defend.

ObjectRealism

Knowledge corresponds to a mind-independent reality. Truth = correspondence.

Problem: no theory-free way to compare language with the world → "pure" objectivity is an illusion.

KnowerIdealism / relativism

The world as known is a creation of the mind; knowledge is a subjective construction. Truth = coherence.

Tends to relativism: "anything goes"; social constructionism is a close cousin.

BothPragmatism ★

The authors' own view. Knowledge is functional & interactive — "coping with the world rather than mirroring it" (Rorty).

Subject-relative but not arbitrary; meaning comes from practical use.
3
The reasoning toolkitdeduction · induction (& its problem) · abduction

To turn observations into knowledge claims, scientists use three kinds of argument. Only the first is conclusive.

CertainDeduction

Conclusion is contained in the premises. If valid + premises true → conclusion must be true.

Euclid, Descartes' dream of deducing all science; Hull's failed formal psychology.

ProbableInduction

Generalise from instances: what holds for some likely holds for all. Support comes by degrees with more evidence.

The engine of empirical science — but never conclusive.

Best guessAbduction

Inference to the best explanation (Peirce): pick the hypothesis that best explains the facts.

"The fridge is open → my partner left it" — plausible, not certain.

CautionThe Problem of Induction (Hume)

Induction cannot be logically justified — the conclusion always goes beyond the evidence, and "nature is uniform" is itself an inductive claim (circular). We infer by habit, not logic.

Raven paradox: a white sneaker "confirms" all ravens are black → the logic breaks down. Pragmatic reply: science succeeds anyway.

Discovery vs. justification: where ideas come from (psychology, accident, Newton's apple) is separated from whether the finished theory is justified. Naturalistic epistemology (Quine) blurs this line.

4
Observation, theory, lawtheory-ladenness · theory & model · empirical vs theoretical laws

Empiricists (Bacon) hoped for pure observation — collect facts and laws emerge automatically. But there is no theory-neutral seeing.

Key ideaTheory-ladenness

Observation is always shaped by theoretical assumptions. A "fact" is a belief held to be true, never simply read off the world.

→ theories are "under-determined by data": several can fit the same data.

DefineTheory & model

Theory (De Groot): a system of logically interrelated, non-contradictory statements from which testable hypotheses can be derived.

Model: a mini-theory / visualisable analogy that mediates between laws and phenomena.

Two kindsEmpirical vs. theoretical laws

Empirical law: only observables (Mendelian inheritance of eye colour).

Theoretical law: includes unobservables (genes, chromosomes) → deeper unification.

Law vs. accidental generalisation: a real law supports counterfactuals (heated copper expands — even on the moon).
5
Causalityregularity view · INUS · causes vs correlations · interventionism

What makes a law explanatory? The intuition that the cause necessitates the effect — but causality is notoriously hard to pin down.

HumeRegularity view

Cause = constant conjunction. The felt "necessity" is in our heads, not observed in the world.

RefinementINUS condition

A cause is an Insufficient but Non-redundant part of an Unnecessary but Sufficient condition.

Short circuit → farm fire: not sufficient alone, not the only possible cause, but non-redundant.

Watch outCause ≠ correlation

Accidental correlations (lampposts & colon cancer), common causes (smoking → yellow fingers and cancer), multiple & circular causation.

Nagel4 conditions for causal laws

Invariable relation · spatial contact / chain · cause precedes & is close to effect · the relation is asymmetrical.

WoodwardInterventionism

A cause is a point of intervention: what would change the effect if we manipulated it. Ties causation to the experiment.

7
The ethos of science — CUDOSMerton's norms · Turner & McCreery · the counternorms

Beyond logic, science runs on a shared ethos. Merton (1942) framed it as four norms — the acronym CUDOS — born from defending science's autonomy against Nazi "Jewish science".

CCommunism

Findings are communal property — a common heritage. Reward = recognition; "standing on the shoulders of giants".

UUniversalism

Claims judged by impersonal criteria, never by the race or status of the claimant.

DDisinterestedness

Acting for the common good, not self-interest; fraud is rare thanks to science's rigorous policing.

O(S)Organized Skepticism

Suspend judgment; scrutinise all beliefs by empirical & logical criteria — a source of friction with religion.

+OOriginality (Ziman)

Modern versions read the O as originality; "replication" is sometimes called a fifth norm.

CritiqueThe counternorms

Mitroff: each norm has a counternorm — e.g. organised dogmatism (defend your own idea fiercely). Mulkay: the norms are an ideology, not real rules.
